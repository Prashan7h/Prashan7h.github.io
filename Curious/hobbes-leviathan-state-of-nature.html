Hobbes' Leviathan and the State of Nature

In 1651, in exile from an English civil war, Thomas Hobbes wrote the first modern argument for the state — and grounded it in a thought experiment about what life would look like without one.

Most political theories begin by describing a good society and asking how to arrange it. Thomas Hobbes did the opposite. He began by describing a world with no society at all — no laws, no courts, no shared authority — and asked what people, being what they are, would do to each other in it. His answer became the founding argument of modern political philosophy: the state, whatever its faults, is what stands between us and something much worse.

Leviathan, published in 1651, is the book where he made that case. It was written in exile in Paris, while his own country tore itself apart in civil war. Hobbes had seen a functioning kingdom collapse into two decades of factional violence, and he was trying to understand, at the deepest level, why we bother with rulers at all.

The state of nature

Hobbes' method is to strip humans of everything social and see what remains. Imagine a world without government — no police, no property law, no shared referee. Two claims then do all the work.

The first is that people are, on the whole, roughly equal. Not identical — some are stronger, some cleverer — but no one is so far ahead that they cannot, on a bad night, be killed by the weakest opponent with a stone or a sharpened stick. Equality here is equality of vulnerability.

The second is that in any world where resources are contestable and futures are uncertain, three motives push people into conflict: competition (for the same scarce thing), diffidence (fear of being attacked first), and glory (reputation, honor, being seen as not-a-pushover). Each of these on its own is enough to make attacking a neighbour a rational move. Together, they make it almost inevitable.

Put these two together and you get a grim equilibrium. Everyone has reason to strike first, because everyone has reason to think the others might. Trust cannot get off the ground. No one plants crops they might not live to harvest; no one builds what someone stronger will take. Hobbes' famous summary:

“In such condition, there is no place for industry… no arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear, and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.”

This is not a claim that people are evil. It is a claim about game theory before the phrase existed. Given the payoffs, cooperation unravels.

Hobbes' move: swap a symmetric web of mutual threat for an asymmetric structure with one authority above all.

The covenant and the sovereign

How do you escape? Not, Hobbes argues, by making everyone nicer. You escape by changing the game. Individuals agree, each with each, to lay down their right to do whatever they judge necessary for self-preservation — provided everyone else does the same — and to transfer that right to a common authority. This is the covenant. The authority it creates is the sovereign: a single decision-maker (a person, an assembly) whose word is enforced by the combined power that everyone has just handed over.

The name of the book comes from what this creature is. The Leviathan is an “artificial man,” a “mortal god,” assembled out of the wills of its subjects for their own protection. The famous frontispiece — a giant king rising over the countryside, his body composed of tiny individual figures — is the argument in a picture. The state is not something external imposed on us. It is us, rearranged.

Hobbes is uncompromising about what this requires. The sovereign has to be undivided — a divided sovereign is really two sovereigns, i.e. no sovereign at all — and its authority has to be near-absolute, because the point of the exercise is to be a court of last resort no one can appeal past. The only right subjects retain, in his stringent version, is the right of self-preservation itself: no one can be obliged to walk voluntarily to their own execution.

This is the part that has made readers uneasy for four hundred years. It reads, at first, like a defense of tyranny. It isn't quite. Hobbes' claim is narrower and more interesting: any effective political order requires a final decider, and pretending otherwise is what plunges you back into civil war. Better a decent Leviathan than none.

Why it broke through

Look at what Hobbes has done, structurally. He has justified political authority without appealing to God, tradition, or the divine right of kings — the standard scaffolding of his century. Instead he has built the argument from below: from claims about individual psychology, individual interests, and what those interests, reasoned about carefully, imply. The state is legitimate because a rational person, thinking clearly about their own survival, would consent to it.

This is the invention of the social contract as a genre. Locke would use the same frame to argue for limited government and a right of revolution; Rousseau would use it to argue that legitimate authority comes from a general will; Rawls, three centuries later, would use it to derive principles of justice from behind a veil of ignorance. All of them are Hobbes' descendants, even where they disagree with him violently. The move — ground the state in a hypothetical agreement between free, roughly equal individuals — is his.

Why the state of nature is unstable. A prisoner's-dilemma-shaped problem three centuries before the phrase.

What Hobbes still teaches

Modern liberal readers usually part company with Hobbes on the absoluteness of the sovereign. We've learned, painfully, that unchecked authority produces its own horrors, and we've built constitutions, rights, and separations of power specifically to blunt it. Hobbes underweighted that risk. He was, understandably, more afraid of the failure mode he had just lived through.

But the deep insight survives. Politics is not fundamentally about which policies are wisest. It is first about how a group of people, aware that they disagree and cannot fully trust each other, manages to live in the same territory without falling into violence. Everything else — markets, science, art, ordinary friendship — requires that this problem be solved first. Hobbes is the philosopher who made this precedence visible.

Read him after a functioning state has been going quietly about its business for a long time and he sounds paranoid. Read him during a civil war, or on the day a state visibly fails, and he sounds like a man who has noticed something the rest of us prefer to forget.

Kant's Categorical Imperative

An 18th-century philosopher in Königsberg tried to derive the whole of morality from a single test that pure reason can run, with no help from God, custom, or feeling.

Most attempts to ground morality begin with something outside reason. With commandments handed down. With the pursuit of happiness. With sympathy, or social contract, or the long shadow of evolved emotion. Immanuel Kant thought none of this would do. If morality depends on what we happen to want, or on who happened to issue the orders, it has no real authority. A good action would just be a fortunate one. Kant wanted something firmer — a moral law that any rational being, anywhere in the universe, could in principle discover for themselves, simply by thinking clearly.

The instrument he invented for this is the categorical imperative. It is one of the most ambitious ideas in the history of philosophy and one of the most often misunderstood.

Hypothetical and categorical

Kant begins by separating two kinds of command. A hypothetical imperative is conditional: if you want to be healthy, exercise; if you want to pass the exam, study. These bind you only insofar as you happen to share the goal. They are everywhere — they are most of what we call practical advice — but they cannot ground morality. The murderer who wants to get away with murder has all the hypothetical reasons in the world to lie, and none of them tells us anything about whether lying is wrong.

A categorical imperative is different. It commands unconditionally. It applies to you because you are a rational agent, full stop, regardless of what you want. The astonishing claim is that such a thing exists at all — that pure practical reason, with no input from desire, can issue any command. Kant thought it could. And he thought there was only one.

The first formulation: the universal law

The most famous version runs:

Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law.

A maxim is the principle behind your action — the rule you'd be following if you described, honestly, what you were doing and why. “Borrow money I cannot repay, by promising falsely to repay it.” “Help the stranger when it costs me nothing.” “Cheat on the exam when I'm sure I won't be caught.”

The test is a thought experiment. Imagine a world in which everyone, in your situation, acted on your maxim — in which it really were a law of nature that they did. Could you coherently will such a world?

The universalization test. The maxim of a false promise destroys the institution it relies on; it cannot be willed as a universal law.

Take the false promise. Suppose your maxim is: when I need money, I will borrow it by promising to repay, even if I know I won't. Universalize it. Now imagine a world in which everyone in financial trouble promises insincerely. In such a world, no one believes promises any more. There is no institution of promising for you to exploit. Your maxim, raised to a universal law, has destroyed the very practice it depends on. It contradicts itself. Therefore, says Kant, you may not act on it.

This is the move that gives the imperative its peculiar feel. It does not appeal to consequences (the loan goes bad, you get caught). It does not appeal to feelings (the lender is hurt). It appeals only to consistency. A wrong action is one that cannot rationally be willed as universal; its very form contains a contradiction.

The second formulation: humanity as an end

Kant insisted the categorical imperative was a single principle, but he restated it in several ways — partly to draw out different aspects, partly because he was a writer who could not resist another angle. The second formulation is, for many readers, the moral heart of the system:

Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never as a means only.

What sets rational beings apart, for Kant, is that we set our own ends. A stone does not have purposes; a wolf has purposes only in the thinnest sense. A person chooses, reflects, plans, and judges. To treat a person merely as a means is to treat them as a stone or a wolf — as something whose ends do not matter. The con artist who deceives, the tyrant who terrorises, the slaver who buys and sells — all do this. They treat the person opposite them as an obstacle or a resource. They ignore the fact that a will lives there.

Two pictures of a transaction. On the left, the other person is a tool whose purposes do not count. On the right, both wills are present.

Notice the careful phrase: as an end and never as a means only. Kant is not saying we cannot use each other. We use each other constantly — we use the barista, the dentist, the colleague. The wrongness is in the only. To use another person while also respecting them as a self-legislating agent — whose consent matters, whose dignity is non-negotiable, whose ends are her own — is permitted. To use them as if they were a thing is not.

What survives, and what doesn't

Kant has had no shortage of critics. Hegel complained that the universalization test is empty: depending on how you describe your maxim, almost anything passes or fails. Bernard Williams thought the relentless impartiality of the system left no room for the personal attachments that give a human life meaning. The famous case of the murderer at the door — Kant seems to insist you may not lie, even to save an innocent — struck even his contemporaries as monstrous, and Kant doubled down on it.

And yet. The categorical imperative, for all its strangeness, has lodged itself permanently in our moral vocabulary. When we say a law should apply to everyone equally, we are speaking Kantian. When we condemn the dictator for treating citizens as instruments of state, we are speaking Kantian. The framers of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights opened with the words “inherent dignity” — a phrase that has no good home outside Kant's vision of persons as ends in themselves. The idea that there are things you may not do to another human being, no matter how good the consequences look, no matter how the calculations balance, is the categorical imperative's long shadow.

What Kant was reaching for — a moral law that any rational mind could discover for itself, owing nothing to scripture, custom, or appetite — remains the most audacious project in ethics. Whether it succeeded is a question the field has not stopped arguing about for two and a half centuries. That, perhaps, is its own kind of answer.
